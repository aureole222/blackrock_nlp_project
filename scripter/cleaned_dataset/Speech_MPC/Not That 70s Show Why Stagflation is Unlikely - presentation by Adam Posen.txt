 For foreign policy , the question facing an intervention decision is whether this is Munich (deter) or Vietnam (accommodate)? For monetary policy, the question is similar but reversed: is this the s (accommodate) or s (deter)? The underlying dynamic today is parallel to the s, but we have avoided repeating that for several reasons: Is there a risk to go from such a state to one of overheating and inflation  or inflation and contraction (aka stagflation)? Bruno and Sachs (), Blanchard and Gali () show that all of them have to interact to (re)produce the s The theoretical literature on central banking (and some press) treats monetary policymaking as under constant suspicion The empirical evidence has not supported this view (Blinder, McCallum, Posen on CBI; Kuttner and Posen on CB governors) A reasonable discourse and accountability, not a  trigger strategy  game of needing to pre empt (fighting the last war?)
